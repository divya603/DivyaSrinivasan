Research

Medical Dialogue Classification

  • During my second year of undergraduate studies, I came across a project offered by the CLEF Initiative (Conference and Labs of the Evaluation Forum). The CLEF Initiative fosters research, innovation, and development by providing a platform for multimodal system testing, model evaluation, the creation of reusable testing frameworks, and the exchange of results.
  • The primary objective of the task was to accurately identify the topic from each snippet of a doctor-patient conversation. Given a fixed list of 20 potential topics or section headers (e.g., Assessment, Diagnosis, Exam, Medications, Past Medical History), the model was designed to determine which category best matched each snippet. By properly classifying these snippets, the aim was to streamline how clinical conversations are organized, making it easier for healthcare professionals and researchers to analyze and review the content.In the project, I developed a machine learning pipeline to classify medical dialogue snippets into predefined section headers.
  • Using a CSV dataset of doctor-patient conversations, the data was split into training, validation, and test sets. After performing key preprocessing steps such as digit and punctuation removal, stopword filtering, and lowercase conversion, a linear Support Vector Classifier (SVC) model was trained with random oversampling to address class imbalance. The model was then validated and used to predict the appropriate section headers on unseen test samples, demonstrating a practical approach for organizing and summarizing clinical notes.

Driver Behavior Profiling-Undergrad Thesis Work

  • For my thesis, I collaborated with The University of Catania under the guidance of Dr.Joannes Sam Mertens, Assistant Professor at University of Catania. The project focused on driver behavior profiling and analysis, a critical aspect of road safety and security applications.
  • Utilizing data collected from sensors installed in vehicles, the project proposed an approach to create unique driving profiles for each user. The research explored the use of deep learning autoencoders, including Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks and Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN), to learn patterns in driving behavior and detect irregularities compared to benchmark models.
  • By analyzing temporal dependencies and unique features, the models assessed similarities and deviations between trained driving profiles and new drivers using the same vehicle. The methodology was validated on two datasets: one collected from bike riders in urban settings and another from car drivers on motorways. Experimental results demonstrated the effectiveness of the approach in both scenarios, highlighting its potential application in various safety contexts and vehicular environments.
